Early Life and Career

Born in Berlin, Germany in 1923, Joseph Weizenbaum and his Jewish family fled the Nazi regime in 1935, immigrating to the United States. He served in the U.S. military during World War II and later studied mathematics. His career path led him to MIT, where he became a professor and a pioneer in the field of artificial intelligence.

The ELIZA Program

In the mid-1960s, Weizenbaum created ELIZA, an early natural language processing program. It simulated a Rogerian psychotherapist by using simple pattern-matching to respond to user statements. Weizenbaum was shocked to see how readily people anthropomorphized the program, confiding in it as if it were a real person.

A Warning to the AI Community

The phenomenon of people over-attributing intelligence to a machine became known as the "ELIZA effect." Weizenbaum's subsequent book, "Computer Power and Human Reason," became a landmark critique of AI, arguing that while computers could be powerful tools, they should never be given the authority to make decisions requiring human values, wisdom, and judgment.

Influence and Legacy

Weizenbaum's work cemented his place as a foundational figure in AI ethics. His warnings about the overreliance on technology and the moral limitations of machines remain profoundly relevant today in discussions about autonomous weapons, privacy, and algorithmic bias. He taught that the power of technology comes with a responsibility to consider its social and human consequences.
